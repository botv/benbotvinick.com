Why I Dropped My Classes


November 2020

I've made certain decisions in the past two months that have completely altered the course of my life.

Before I begin to explain, I'd like to acknowledge my motivations here. By nature, I'm not a very public person. I actually much prefer that nobody around me has any kind of window into my life, except for my closest friends. To that end, I've kept very quiet over the past few months, and have, at some points, been deliberately misleading when people have asked me why I dropped three classes and completely withdrew myself socially. There came a point when I realized that my current life situation is so tightly coupled with my identity that it's become difficult to avoid an explanation in casual conversations. So my motivations here are simple: 1. to avoid the tedious explanation of my life situation that has become so unavoidable, 2. to get ahead of any rumors you hyenas have been cooking up, and 3. to give you a better idea of why I act the way I do. If this essay comes across as even a little bit self-indulgent, please text me, and we can hash it out. Because that's the last thing I want. My number is (215) 880-4458.

To understand why I dropped my classes, you first have to understand why I switched schools after freshman year. Although being sick of GFS after ten years of meeting for worship (the Quaker practice of gathering as a community for an extended period of silent reflection) had something to do with it, my transfer to Milton was much more heavily motivated by reckless adventurism. As someone who's counted the days until high school graduation since middle school, I was convinced that living in a dorm would be the perfect way of attaining the independence I longed for. [1] During my first two years at Milton, I pursued every opportunity, made every connection, and took every risk I possibly could have taken. Acting again out of an immense desire to transcend high school, I became a freelance web developer, held positions at various companies, cofounded two shitty startups (both of which inevitably failed after a few months of pickledicking around), and eventually settled into a full-time position at a company I won't name here for reasons I won't get into. The value of these experiences was unparalleled, and as I entered college season — or as one of my friends describes it, "The Jungle" — in the spring of 2020, I was sure that I had done everything I possibly could have done in high school to set myself up for a happy life, and the time to move on had finally arrived.

Then along came COVID. In the interest of brevity, I will pretend here as if the pandemic's only effect was to bestow upon me an abundance of free time, the likes of which I had never experienced before. [2] I filled this void in a number of ways, including reconnecting with old friends by spending an inordinate amount of time watching repugnant films (e.g. The Kissing Booth) and then critiquing them for hours on end. [3] During the day, I began working on a side project building payment infrastructure for online businesses.

My goal for the summer was not to found a startup. My goal for the summer was not to deal with lawyers, hire employees, and write code for twelve hours a day, every day. My goal for the summer was not to facilitate half a million dollars in consumer transactions in eighteen different countries for five hundred small businesses. None of these things were on my summer 2020 bucket list. But they all happened, and by August I was in way over my head.

In late September, after working over twelve hours a day for six months, I was offered venture capital to pursue the project full-time. [4] In hopes of preserving whatever privacy I have left, I will not state the precise amount I was offered, nor by whom (if these details are important to you, feel free to text me, my number is listed in the second paragraph). However, because I'm sure that many of you are convinced I'm a drama queen at this point, I feel inclined to disclose that the official valuation of the company is on the order of a few million dollars. [5]

Although I never meant or expected to end up doing what I'm doing, I'm happier than a pig in shit. I get to work with the smartest people in the world building a product for customers I love to death, and I get to do it on my terms. I wake up in the morning and feel like the captain of a ship I built. But taking money from an investor raises the stakes significantly. If I fail, I have to clean up the mess, and if I throw in the towel, I sacrifice any hope of raising money from another respectable investor in the future. If I've learned anything since March, it's that Hollywood lied. Running a startup is by far the hardest thing I've ever had to do, and the 99% chance of failure goes to show just how insane I am to be doing it at all.

The day after graduation, I'm flying out to California and I'm not coming back until I fail. The catch, and the reason I'm writing this essay in the first place, is that failure is not the worst-case scenario in my mind. In reality, my nightmare is waking up five years from now and realizing that nobody in my life has any idea who I am. This essay is step one in my five-year plan to prevent that from happening.

If even one of you read to this point and knows any more about me than you did before, then this was a success. Again, my number is (215) 880-4458. Don't be a stranger.
